Gradient Descent

How a machine finds the bottom of a valley it can't see.

Imagine you're dropped somewhere on a foggy hillside at night. You can't see the valley floor — but you can feel which way the ground tilts under your feet. So you take a step downhill. Then you feel again, and step again. Do this enough times and you reach the bottom without ever seeing the whole landscape. That is the entire idea behind how most modern AI learns. The only real question is: how big should each step be? Slide the control below and watch.

The intuition, then the formula

The hillside is a loss function — a number that says how wrong the model currently is. Here we use a deliberately simple one so you can see everything at once:

J(\theta) = (\theta - 3)^2 + 2

Its lowest point is at , where the loss bottoms out at 2. The model doesn't know that. All it can compute is the slope — the gradient — which for this curve is . The slope points uphill, so to go down we step in the opposite direction, scaled by a step size called the learning rate:

\theta \leftarrow \theta - \alpha \, \nabla J(\theta)

That one line, repeated, is gradient descent. Everything interesting is hiding in the choice of — which is exactly what the slider changes.

Three regimes — and why they happen

Subtract 3 from both sides of the update and the structure pops out: . Each step multiplies your distance-to-the-bottom by the factor . That single factor decides your fate, and it's why the three regimes below are not three behaviors — they're one behavior seen at three sizes.

converging

α ≲ 0.5. The factor is between 0 and 1, so each step shrinks the gap. You glide down and settle. Small α is safe but slow — many tiny steps.

overshooting

0.5 ≲ α < 1. The factor goes negative: you leap past the bottom and land on the far wall, then bounce back, overshooting each time — but by less. It still converges, zig-zagging in.

diverging

α ≥ 1. Now the factor's size is ≥ 1: every bounce is as big or bigger. The ball climbs the walls and flies out of the valley. The loss explodes to infinity. Too-eager steps make learning worse, not faster.

Why this toy curve runs the real world

In 2012 a network called AlexNet won the ImageNet competition by a stunning margin — a top-5 error of about 15.3% versus 26.2% for the runner-up — and effectively launched the deep-learning era. Under the hood it was trained with stochastic gradient descent: the same you're playing with, applied not to one parabola but to ~60 million parameters, with the gradient estimated from small random batches of images (that's the “stochastic” part). The team used a learning rate of 0.01 and manually divided it by 10 whenever progress stalled — a hand-tuned version of the exact tradeoff your slider exposes: too big and it diverges, too small and it crawls. [Krizhevsky, Sutskever & Hinton, NeurIPS 2012]

Curiosity: gradient descent isn't new AI — it's 1847. The French mathematician Augustin-Louis Cauchy proposed it to solve astronomy equations, more than a century before the first neural network. [history]

Curiosity: real loss landscapes have millions of dimensions, so they're full of saddle points — places flat in some directions and steep in others. Counterintuitively, in very high dimensions almost all “traps” are saddles, not bad valleys, which is part of why descent works as well as it does.

The one thing to remember

Learning is just feel the slope, step downhill, repeat — and the size of that step is the whole ballgame. Too timid and you never arrive; too bold and you fly off the map; the art is the Goldilocks rate in between. The fog-bound hiker from the start was the model all along.
